Syscribe is a documentation platform for teams that ship faster than they can write. It watches your pull requests, drafts the changelog entry, the user-facing release note, and (optionally) the internal Loom-style summary — then waits for a human to approve before anything is published.

I started building it because every engineering org I've worked in has the same broken loop: PRs get merged, documentation drifts, and within six months the internal wiki is a lie. The teams that solve it spend a real percentage of their cycle time writing release notes by hand. That cost is invisible until you measure it.

### What's hard

The model is not the hard part. Routing is. Knowing which changes deserve a customer-facing line, which are internal-only, and which should be silently swallowed is mostly a taxonomy problem and partly a context problem. We're solving it with a small classifier that runs ahead of the writer model, and a per-repo policy that gets richer the more it ships.

### What I'm learning

Selling to small engineering teams is a different motion to anything I've done. Buying committees of one. Cycles measured in days, not quarters. I've talked to thirty-eight teams in the last six weeks and the pattern is consistent enough that I trust it.
